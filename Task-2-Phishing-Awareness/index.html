Detailed Definitions & Core Types

To fight phishing, you must first understand the taxonomy of the attack. It is not just about fake emails; it is a diverse ecosystem of deception.

1. Spear Phishing

A highly targeted attack aimed at a specific individual or organization. Attackers research the victim (on LinkedIn/Twitter) to include personal details like your job title or boss's name.

2. Whaling

A form of spear phishing aimed at "big fish" like CEOs, CFOs, or high-level executives to steal sensitive corporate secrets or initiate huge wire transfers.

3. Smishing & Vishing

Smishing is phishing via SMS (Text messages). Vishing is voice phishing (phone calls) where attackers use "deepfake" voices or urgent tones to scare victims.

4. Pharming

A technical attack that redirects a website's traffic to a fake site without the victim's knowledge by poisoning the DNS (Domain Name System).

Technical Indicators: Deep Analysis

Attackers rely on you being in a hurry. If you pause for 10 seconds, you can spot these technical inconsistencies.

🔍 URL Dissection (Typosquatting)

Attackers register domains that look almost identical to real ones. Look for these tricks:

  • micros0ft.com (Zero instead of an O)
  • paypa1.com (Number 1 instead of an L)
  • support.apple-security.net (Adding extra hyphens or changing .com to .net)

📧 Header Manipulations

Check the "From" address. A display name can say "Microsoft Support," but the actual email address might be xyz123@gmail.com. Legitimate companies always use their own official domain.

Social Engineering Tactics

The "Hack" doesn't happen on the computer; it happens in the victim's brain. Attackers use these psychological triggers:

Urgency

"Your account will be deleted in 2 hours!" By creating a ticking clock, attackers disable your critical thinking.

Authority

Posing as the CEO or an IT Director. Most people are conditioned to follow orders from authority figures without questioning them.

Fear & Intimidation

Threatening legal action or financial loss. Fear is the strongest human emotion used to force a "click."

Curiosity/Greed

"Click here to see your salary bonus!" or "Package tracking details attached." People love good news or resolving mysteries.

Prevention & Best Practices

Becoming a "Human Firewall" requires a multi-layered defense strategy.

✅ Defensive Checklist

  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Even if they get your password, they can't get in without your physical phone code.
  • Hover Before You Click: On a computer, hover your mouse over a link to see the "hidden" URL in the bottom corner of your screen.
  • Second-Channel Verification: If your "Boss" asks for a strange favor via email, call them on the phone to confirm it was really them.
  • Password Managers: Use unique, 20-character passwords for every site.
