Detailed Definitions & Core Types

To fight phishing, you must first understand the taxonomy of the attack. It is not just about fake emails; it is a diverse ecosystem of deception.

1. Spear Phishing

A highly targeted attack aimed at a specific individual or organization. Attackers research the victim on professional networks to include custom tailored details.

2. Whaling

A form of spear phishing aimed at high-profile targets like CEOs and CFOs to authorize fraudulent financial operations or yield sensitive trade secrets.

3. Smishing & Vishing

Smishing uses SMS text channels to deliver malicious links. Vishing relies on telephone calls, often manipulating caller ID info or voice syntax.

4. Pharming

A technical manipulation that hijacks DNS records or system configurations, redirecting victims to rogue domains even if the correct address was typed.

Technical Indicators

Attackers rely on you being in a hurry. If you pause for 10 seconds, you can spot these technical inconsistencies.

🔍 URL Dissection (Typosquatting)

Attackers register domains that look almost identical to real ones. Look for these tricks:

  • micros0ft.com (Zero instead of an O)
  • paypa1.com (Number 1 instead of an L)
  • support.apple-security.net (Adding extra hyphens or changing suffixes)

📧 Header Manipulations

Check the "From" address. A display name can say "Corporate IT," but the actual tracking sender address might point to a generic external utility provider domain.

Social Engineering Tactics

The "Hack" doesn't happen on the computer; it happens in the victim's brain. Attackers use these psychological triggers:

Urgency

By creating a strict, short timeline, attackers compromise rational thought processes, pressuring targets to take shortcuts.

Authority

Impersonating executive profiles or legal agencies to take advantage of natural tendencies to comply with operational hierarchies.

Fear & Intimidation

Threatening direct loss of system functionality, monetary adjustments, or professional exposure to force structural compliance.

Curiosity / Greed

Dangling unexpected incentives, corporate bonuses, or tracking confirmations to trigger impulsive link exploration behavior.

Prevention & Best Practices

Becoming a "Human Firewall" requires a multi-layered defense strategy.

✅ Defensive Checklist

  • Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Prevents unauthorized credential use even if static passwords become exposed.
  • Hover Verification Rules: Always preview underlying targets before establishing active digital connections.
  • Secondary Verification Channels: Validate anomalous out-of-band operational requests through distinct secondary communication lines.
  • Password Vault Infrastructures: Implement complex, distinct account boundaries across corporate environments.

Reporting Phishing

Defensive responses matter. If you suspect an anomaly or identify an incoming threat vector, execute the following operational sequence:

🛡️ Incident Protocol Response

  • Don't click links: Bypasses initial attack executions entirely.
  • Don't download attachments: Prevents unauthorized endpoint code executions or payload drops.
  • Report to IT / Security Team: Forwards metadata footprints so security teams can insulate corporate perimeter firewalls.
  • Delete the email: Clears threat vectors out of your active workspace environment.
